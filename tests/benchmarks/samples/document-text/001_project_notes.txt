Project notes: onboarding flow redesign
=========================================

Attendees: growth team, design, one engineer from platform
Date context: mid-quarter check-in, second of three planned sessions

Summary of where we are

The current onboarding flow has seven distinct steps between account creation and a user
reaching the main dashboard for the first time. Analytics from the last full quarter show
that roughly forty percent of new signups do not complete all seven steps, and the single
largest drop-off point is step four, the workspace-naming screen, which for reasons nobody
on the growth team can fully explain, has a noticeably higher abandonment rate than either
the step before it or the step after it. We spent a good portion of this session trying to
understand why that particular screen is so much worse than its neighbors, and did not
reach a firm conclusion, which is itself useful information: it means the problem is
probably not obvious from looking at the screen alone, and we should instrument it more
before redesigning it blind.

Several people raised the possibility that the workspace-naming screen is simply the first
point in the flow where a user is asked to make a decision that feels permanent, as opposed
to the earlier steps, which are all either informational or trivially reversible. If that
theory holds, the fix might not be about the screen's visual design at all, but about
reducing the perceived permanence of the choice, for example by making it clear the
workspace name can be changed later with no consequences. Nobody in the room was confident
enough in this theory to commit engineering time to it yet, so the next step is a small
user-research pass specifically targeting that screen before any redesign work begins.

Decisions made this session

Decision: we will not attempt to reduce the number of steps in the flow this quarter. Two
earlier attempts to compress steps together (in the two quarters before this one) both
made completion rates worse, not better, and the working theory is that compressing steps
increases the cognitive load of each remaining step even as it reduces the total count,
which is a wash at best. We are treating "reduce total steps" as a dead end until someone
has a genuinely new idea for why it would work differently this time.

Decision: instrumentation for the workspace-naming screen specifically will ship before
the end of this month, ahead of any other onboarding work, because right now we are
redesigning based on a hypothesis rather than data, and that is not how we want to spend
engineering time on a flow this important.

Requirements captured

REQ-201: the instrumentation must capture, at minimum, time spent on the workspace-naming
screen, whether the user changed the auto-suggested name or kept it, and whether the user
navigated backward from this screen at any point before eventually completing it or
abandoning the flow entirely.

REQ-202: none of the new instrumentation may add a new network round-trip to the critical
path of the onboarding flow itself — it must be fire-and-forget, batched, and non-blocking,
because onboarding flow latency was itself the subject of a previous project and we do not
want to regress the work that project already did.

REQ-203: any user-research recruitment for the follow-up study must exclude users who have
already completed onboarding within the last thirty days, to avoid recall bias from asking
people to describe a decision they made a long time ago and may not remember accurately.

Miscellaneous discussion, not yet actionable

There was a tangential conversation about whether the seven-step flow should eventually be
replaced with a fundamentally different pattern (for example, letting users reach a
functional-but-unconfigured dashboard immediately, and prompting for the remaining setup
steps contextually as they're needed) rather than front-loading everything before the user
sees any value at all. Nobody thinks this is worth pursuing this quarter given the decision
above to focus on the workspace-naming screen specifically, but it was flagged as a
reasonable idea to revisit once the current, narrower investigation concludes one way or
another. WARNING: if we do pursue this eventually, it is a much larger project than
anything discussed in this session and would need its own dedicated planning process rather
than being folded into ongoing onboarding-flow iteration.

Attendance and logistics note: two people who normally attend these sessions were out this
week, and their input on the workspace-naming theory specifically would be valuable given
their prior involvement in the original design of that screen. Their feedback will be
gathered asynchronously before the next session rather than delaying this round of notes.

Background context from the first session, carried forward for continuity

For anyone reading these notes without having attended the first session, a short recap:
the onboarding-flow project was originally scoped much more broadly, covering not just the
seven-step signup flow but also the first-week email sequence and the in-app tooltip tour
that fires the first time a user reaches the dashboard. That broader scope was deliberately
narrowed after the first session specifically because the data available at the time was
strong enough to justify focusing on the signup flow alone (the forty percent drop-off
figure cited above) but much weaker for the email sequence and tooltip tour, where nobody
could point to a specific metric showing either one was actually a problem worth solving
right now. The decision to narrow scope was not a statement that the email sequence or
tooltip tour are fine as they are — it was a statement that we don't yet know whether they
are fine, and spending this quarter's engineering time on the one place where we do have
strong evidence of a problem is the better bet. This should be revisited once the current
narrower project concludes, not forgotten entirely.

There was also a longer discussion in the first session about measurement methodology that
is worth summarizing here for continuity, since it shapes how the forty percent figure
should be interpreted. The number is calculated as: of all accounts created in the quarter,
what fraction never reached the dashboard at all, at any point, even days later. This is a
deliberately generous definition of "completed" — a user who abandoned the flow on day one
and came back three weeks later to finish it still counts as having completed it, not
dropped off. A stricter same-session completion metric was considered and rejected, because
the team judged that a user who eventually gets there, even slowly, represents a
meaningfully different (and less urgent) problem than a user who never gets there at all,
and blending the two into one number would make it harder to tell which problem the forty
percent figure is actually describing.

One more piece of carried-forward context: the workspace-naming screen was not originally
suspected to be the primary drop-off point going into the first session. The initial
hypothesis, based on nothing more than intuition, was that the very first step (email
verification) would be the worst offender, since email verification is a well-known
industry-wide source of drop-off across many products, not specific to this one. The actual
data surprised the room — email verification drop-off is below the flow's average, not
above it, and the workspace-naming screen is the clear outlier. This is exactly why the
team is being cautious about acting on intuition alone this time around, and why the
instrumentation-first approach was chosen over jumping straight to a redesign based on a
plausible-sounding but previously wrong guess.

Additional context on why this matters beyond this one flow

It is worth spending a paragraph on why this particular project has gotten more attention
from leadership than a typical mid-sized onboarding tweak usually would. Signup completion
rate has been cited in the last two company-wide planning reviews as one of a small number
of metrics that leadership is tracking closely this year, alongside retention and expansion
revenue, and this is the single largest concrete lever the growth team currently has
identified for moving that number. That context does not change anything about the
technical approach described above, but it does explain why this project has been given
priority over several other reasonable candidates the growth team could otherwise have
picked this quarter, and why the team is being unusually careful about not shipping a
redesign based on an unverified hypothesis, given how much visibility the outcome will have.

There is a reasonable question of whether this level of process — instrumentation before
redesign, a dedicated research pass, careful measurement-methodology discussion — is
overkill for what is, after all, a single screen in a signup flow. The counterargument that
carried the room is that the team has already spent two prior quarters on this same general
problem without a durable improvement, specifically because past attempts skipped the
instrumentation step and went straight to plausible-sounding redesigns that did not hold up
once shipped. Repeating that pattern a third time, on a problem important enough to be
tracked at the leadership level, was judged not worth the risk of another quarter with
nothing durable to show for it, even though the more careful approach will visibly take
longer to produce a shipped change. This tradeoff was discussed explicitly and everyone in
the room agreed it was the right call given the last two quarters' track record, even
though it means leadership will need to be told, again, that a concrete redesign is still a
quarter or more away rather than landing immediately.
